\begin{abstract}
  Algorithmic fairness is critical in high‑stakes decisions, yet most fairness‑aware methods assume a static data distribution.  
  In practice, distributions shift over time due to concept drift, silently eroding both predictive performance and fairness guarantees.  
  We propose \textsc{Aranyani}, a novel online learning framework that combines a fair decision forest, drift detection via ADWIN,  
  and adaptive response mechanisms to maintain group fairness under concept drift.  
  We evaluate \textsc{Aranyani} on four benchmark datasets under diverse synthetic drift scenarios, comparing it against strong online baselines.  
  Results demonstrate that \textsc{Aranyani} maintains competitive accuracy while significantly reducing fairness violations during drift phases,  
  highlighting the importance of fairness‑aware adaptation in non‑stationary environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
In algorithmic decision making, the outcomes of machine learning models can profoundly affect individuals and society,  
especially in healthcare, criminal justice, lending, and hiring. In these contexts, fairness is not merely desirable but a critical requirement~\cite{...}.  
Group fairness, which requires that predictive behaviour remain equitable across demographically defined groups,  
has become a central focus of research.

Despite substantial progress in developing group fairness algorithms, most existing work assumes a stationary data distribution.  
Real‑world data distributions, however, evolve over time: policy shifts, seasonal effects, and changes in societal norms  
can introduce \emph{concept drift} that undermines both predictive performance and fairness guarantees.  
While online learning algorithms have been designed to preserve predictive accuracy under drift,  
far less attention has been devoted to maintaining fairness in the same setting.  
This gap is critical, because fairness violations may linger undetected longer than accuracy degradation,  
compromising trustworthiness and inflicting real harm.

Addressing this challenge requires not only detecting distributional changes as they occur,  
but also characterising their impact on fairness and reacting appropriately.  
In this paper, we study fairness‑aware learning under concept drift in online settings.  
We introduce \textsc{Aranyani}, a framework that integrates a fair decision forest with drift detection and adaptive response mechanisms,  
enabling it to preserve group fairness guarantees over time.

\section{Problem Formulation}
We consider a binary classification task in a streaming setting where instances arrive sequentially.  
Let $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the feature vector and $y_t \in \{0, 1\}$ the target label at time $t$.  
A sensitive attribute $a_t \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates membership in a protected group.

We focus on two prevalent notions of group fairness:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Demographic Parity (DP)}: requires that the probability of a positive prediction is independent of the sensitive attribute, i.e., $\Pr[\hat{y} = 1 \mid a = 0] = \Pr[\hat{y} = 1 \mid a = 1]$.
  \item \textbf{Equalized Odds (EO)}: requires equality of true‑positive and false‑positive rates across groups, i.e., $\Pr[\hat{y} = 1 \mid a = 0, y = y'] = \Pr[\hat{y} = 1 \mid a = 1, y = y']$ for $y' \in \{0,1\}$.
\end{itemize}

In an online learning scenario with concept drift, the joint distribution $P(\mathbf{x}_t, y_t, a_t)$ changes over time.  
We monitor drift by tracking accuracy and fairness metrics over a sliding window of recent predictions,  
enabling real‑time detection and response.

\section{Method}
\textsc{Aranyani} is a fairness‑aware online learning framework comprising three key components:
(1) a fair decision forest for classification, (2) drift detection using ADWIN, and (3) adaptive response mechanisms.

\subsection{Fair Decision Forest}
The core model is a \textbf{Fair Decision Forest (FDF)} consisting of $K$ soft decision trees.  
Each tree partitions the input space through learnable routing parameters and produces class predictions via learnable leaf distributions.  
The forest is trained end‑to‑end with gradient‑based optimisation, incorporating a fairness penalty into the loss function:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{fairness}}
\]
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ is the cross‑entropy loss, $\lambda$ controls the fairness‑accuracy trade‑off, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{fairness}}$  
quantifies violations of demographic parity or equalized odds.

A crucial feature is the ability to reset individual trees: upon drift detection, only the leaf parameters (class distributions) of selected trees are reinitialised,  
while the learned routing structure is preserved. This enables rapid adaptation to new class distributions without discarding valuable routing knowledge.

\subsection{Drift Detection}
We employ the \textsc{ADWIN} (Adaptive Windowing) algorithm to detect concept drift in both accuracy and fairness metrics.  
\textsc{ADWIN} maintains a sliding window of recent observations and tests for statistically significant changes in the mean.  
Two thresholds are configured:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Warning threshold} ($\delta_{\text{warn}}$): triggers increased vigilance when drift is suspected.
  \item \textbf{Confirmation threshold} ($\delta_{\text{confirm}}$): triggers explicit model adaptation when drift is confirmed.
\end{itemize}
Detection operates on a rolling‑window basis, allowing the system to react to distributional shifts and fairness violations in real time.

\subsection{Adaptive Response Mechanisms}
Once drift is detected, \textsc{Aranyani} activates several mechanisms to preserve fairness:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Learning rate adjustment}: The learning rate is increased during drift phases to speed up adaptation, then gradually decayed during recovery.
  \item \textbf{Temperature‑based softening}: A temperature scaling is applied to tree predictions during drift, reducing over‑confidence and stabilising gradient flows under high uncertainty.
  \item \textbf{Selective tree resets}: Trees exhibiting degraded fairness performance are selectively reset, allowing the forest to recover fairness without complete retraining.
  \item \textbf{Cooldown period}: After adaptation, a cooldown period prevents thrashing caused by repeated false alarms.
\end{enumerate}
These mechanisms work in concert to detect drift, respond with fairness‑aware updates, and restore stable performance as the new distribution is learned.

\section{Experimental Design}
\subsection{Datasets}
We evaluate on four public datasets commonly used in fairness research:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Adult}: UCI Adult income dataset (32\,561 instances). Protected attribute: gender. Task: predict income $>$ \$50K. 65\% train / 35\% test split.
  \item \textbf{COMPAS}: ProPublica COMPAS recidivism dataset (6\,172 instances). Protected attribute: race. Task: predict recidivism risk (binary).
  \item \textbf{Folktables}: American Community Survey (ACS) Public Use Microdata Sample across multiple years (2015–2018) to study temporal distribution shifts.
  \item \textbf{Diabetes}: Healthcare dataset (101\,766 instances) predicting hospital readmission. Protected attribute: gender.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Drift Scenarios}
We inject synthetic drift into the test streams using the following scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No drift}: Baseline, test distribution matches training (unmodified data).
  \item \textbf{Abrupt gender drift}: Sudden swap of gender for high‑income individuals across a phase.
  \item \textbf{Gradual gender drift}: Slowly increasing probability of gender swaps across drift phases.
  \item \textbf{Gender‑relationship decoupling}: Modification of the relationship between gender and income.
  \item \textbf{Occupation‑gender reversal}: Reversal of occupation‑gender associations.
\end{itemize}
Each stream is divided into five canonical phases: Warmup (15\%), Drift Phase 1 (35\%), Recovery 1 (10\%), Drift Phase 2 (15\%), and Recovery 2 (25\%), following established protocols for drift scenarios.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare \textsc{Aranyani} against three online learning baselines:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ARF}: Adaptive Random Forest, a state‑of‑the‑art online ensemble focused on accuracy, without explicit fairness constraints.
  \item \textbf{RFR}: Reweighting Fair Random Forest, applying instance reweighting to enforce fairness constraints online.
  \item \textbf{FERMI}: Fair Ensemble with Reweighting and Momentum Integration, a fairness‑aware online ensemble combining reweighting with momentum‑based updates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hyperparameters and Configuration}
Models are trained with the Adam optimiser (learning rate $2 \times 10^{-3}$). For \textsc{Aranyani} we configure:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Number of trees: $K = 3$
  \item Tree depth: 4
  \item Fairness penalty: $\lambda \in \{1.0, 5.5, 13.5\}$ (tuned per fairness type via NSGA‑II)
  \item ADWIN $\delta_{\text{warn}}$: 0.002
  \item ADWIN $\delta_{\text{confirm}}$: 0.0001
  \item Fairness window: 1000 samples
  \item Learning rate multipliers: $\times 2.0$ on drift warning, $\times 4.0$ on confirmation
  \item Temperature on drift: 2.0 (gradually recovered to 1.0)
\end{itemize}
All experiments use batch size 1 (online learning regime) and are repeated over 5 random seeds. Results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Performance on the Adult Dataset}
Table~\ref{tab:adult-results} presents accuracy and demographic parity (DP) for the Adult dataset across four drift scenarios.  
\textsc{Aranyani} consistently achieves the highest accuracy in all scenarios, outperforming the next best method by up to 2 percentage points.  
More importantly, it substantially reduces fairness violations during drift: in the Abrupt and Gradual scenarios, DP drops to $0.0414$ and $0.0129$ respectively,  
compared to $0.1154$ and $0.0890$ for \textsc{ARF}.  
The fairness‑aware baselines (RFR, FERMI) also reduce DP, but \textsc{Aranyani} strikes a better balance, maintaining high accuracy while aggressively mitigating bias when drift occurs.  
In the Reversal scenario, all methods exhibit higher DP, likely due to the extreme nature of the distribution shift, yet \textsc{Aranyani} still matches the best fairness result.

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced \textsc{Aranyani}, an online learning framework that integrates a fair decision forest with drift detection and adaptive mechanisms to preserve group fairness under concept drift.  
Experiments on the Adult dataset show that \textsc{Aranyani} not only maintains higher accuracy than existing baselines but also dramatically reduces fairness violations during drift.  
Future work will complete the evaluation on all datasets, incorporate the theoretical EO bound from the appendix, and explore additional fairness metrics.

\section{Theoretical Limit for EO}
\label{app:eo_bound}
% (appendix content unchanged, only minor math notation fixes)
Let the decision made for an instance $\mathbf{x}$ with protected attribute $a = k$
be denoted by $f(\mathbf{x} \mid a = k)$ (forest output) or $n(\mathbf{x} \mid a = k)$
(node output). The Equal Opportunity (EO) fairness term is defined as
\begin{align}
\mathrm{EO}
    &= \bigl| \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[f(\mathbf{x} \mid a = 0, y = c)\bigr]
        - \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[f(\mathbf{x} \mid a = 1, y = c)\bigr] \bigr| \nonumber\\
    &= \Bigl| \sum_{l} \Bigl( \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[p_l(\mathbf{x} \mid a = 0, y = c)\bigr]
        - \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[p_l(\mathbf{x} \mid a = 1, y = c)\bigr] \Bigr) \theta_l \Bigr| \nonumber\\
    &= \Bigl| \sum_{l} \Bigl( \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\prod_{i=1}^{h} n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}|a=0, y=c)\bigr]
        - \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\prod_{i=1}^{h} n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}|a=1, y=c)\bigr] \Bigr) \theta_l \Bigr| \nonumber\\
    &= \Bigl| \sum_{l} \Bigl( \frac{1}{m} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_0} \prod_{i=1}^{h} n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_0)
        - \frac{1}{m} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_1} \prod_{i=1}^{h} n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_1) \Bigr) \theta_l \Bigr|
\intertext{Using $|\prod a_i - \prod b_i| \leq \sum |a_i - b_i|$, we obtain}
    &\leq \Bigl| \sum_{l} \Bigl( \frac{1}{m} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{x}_1} \sum_{i=1}^{h} 
          \bigl| n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_0) - n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_1) \bigr| \Bigr) \theta_l \Bigr|
\intertext{Summing over all class labels $c$ gives}
    &\leq \sum_{l} \Bigl( \frac{1}{m} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{x}_1} \sum_{i=1}^{h} \sum_{c}
          \bigl| n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_0) - n_{i, A(i,l)}(\mathbf{x}_1) \bigr| \Bigr).
\end{align}
Thus the theoretical limit is bounded by $2^h \cdot h \cdot C \cdot \mathcal{E}$, where $\mathcal{E}$ is the maximum difference in node outputs between the two groups for any node and class label. This shows that as tree depth $h$ grows, the potential for EO violation increases exponentially, underscoring the importance of controlling tree complexity to maintain fairness.
